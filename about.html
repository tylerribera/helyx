The Building Blocks of Biological Signaling

Peptides are short chains of amino acids ‚Äî typically between 2 and 50 residues ‚Äî linked by peptide bonds. They are distinguished from proteins primarily by their shorter length, though the boundary is not strictly defined.

In biological systems, peptides serve as critical signaling molecules. They function as hormones, neurotransmitters, and growth factors, modulating nearly every physiological process from immune response to cellular repair and metabolic regulation.

Why Peptides Matter in Modern Research

Peptides occupy a unique space in biochemical research. Unlike small molecules, peptides can interact with targets using large, specific binding surfaces. Unlike full proteins, they are easier and less expensive to synthesize, modify, and study.

This makes them invaluable tools for researchers investigating receptor-ligand interactions, cell signaling cascades, immune modulation, neuroprotection, and metabolic pathways. The pharmaceutical industry has recognized this potential ‚Äî over 80 peptide-based therapeutics have been approved globally, with hundreds more in clinical trials.

How Research Peptides Are Synthesized

Modern research peptides are produced via solid-phase peptide synthesis (SPPS), a method pioneered by Nobel laureate Bruce Merrifield. This technique allows for precise, stepwise assembly of amino acid sequences on a solid resin support.

After synthesis, crude peptides undergo purification via high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) to achieve ‚â•99% purity. Identity is confirmed through mass spectrometry (MS), and each batch receives a Certificate of Analysis documenting purity, identity, and appearance.
